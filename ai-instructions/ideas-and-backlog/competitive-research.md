# Competitive Research & Inspiration

A place to dump observations from other food/drink ordering sites.

## 2026-04-10 - First pass

Looked at: Starbucks, McDonald's (via DoorDash), DoorDash home, Crumbl Cookies

### Starbucks (starbucks.com/menu)
- Left sidebar with drink categories (Protein Beverages, Hot Coffee, Cold Coffee, Matcha, etc.) + Food section
- Main area shows circular product images with category names
- "Trending" and "Energy Refreshers" featured at top
- Clean but A LOT of categories - long sidebar scroll

### McDonald's (mcdonalds.order.online - powered by DoorDash)
- Horizontal tab navigation: Most Ordered, Limited Time, Extra Value Meals, Burgers, Chicken, etc.
- Product cards with image, name, price, like %, and number of ratings
- "Most liked" badges on popular items
- Plus button on each card for quick add

### DoorDash (doordash.com/home)
- Delivery/Pickup toggle top right
- Left sidebar: Grocery, Retail, Catering, Reservations, Late Night, Alcohol, Apparel, etc.
- Weekly Deals carousel with restaurant cards (rating, delivery time, delivery fee, promo)
- Feels like a LOT going on - many categories, many promos

### Crumbl Cookies (crumblcookies.com)
- Pink branding, very clean and focused
- Login via phone number (SMS code)
- Personalized: "Welcome back, Orien" with rewards (Crumbs points)
- Simple product grid: Single, 4-Pack, 6-Pack, 12-Pack with save % badges
- Mini Desserts section below
- Much simpler than the others - fewer choices, more focused

### Overall takeaway
- As a customer, sites like Starbucks and DoorDash feel like TOO MUCH STUFF
- Crumbl's focused, simple approach feels nicer - fewer decisions, cleaner layout
- Something to think about for Baked with Blessings' menu/ordering UX

### Starbucks - what they are strong at

What stands out from the official Starbucks sources:

- broad menu surface, not just cookies or pastries
- coffee ritual first, food second but increasingly important
- strong rewards and repeat-visit machinery
- convenience and habit are a huge part of the value
- globally inspired seasonal or limited food items keep the menu feeling fresh

As of February 5, 2026, Starbucks was explicitly highlighting new food launches like:

- Cookie Croissant Swirl
- Berry Blondie
- 1971 Roast

Strategic takeaway:

- Starbucks is a convenience-and-routine competitor more than a cookie-specialist competitor
- people choose Starbucks when they want a reliable coffee stop, fast pickup, rewards, and broad choice

### Crumbl - what they are strong at

What stands out from the official Crumbl sources:

- extremely focused product framing
- dessert-first, not cafe-first
- weekly rotation drives return visits
- strong packaging and gifting logic
- app, rewards, and box formats make ordering feel simple

The current Crumbl framing is built around:

- six permanent classics plus four rotating weekly flavors
- single-cookie and multi-pack ordering
- rewards tiers and app-driven repeat behavior
- shareable box formats that naturally support group orders and gifting

Strategic takeaway:

- Crumbl wins on novelty, indulgence, and shareability
- people choose Crumbl when they want a big, rich, photogenic dessert moment

### Price comparison reality

Important note:

- Starbucks does not expose a simple national public pricing feed
- Crumbl pricing is also store-sensitive and bundle-sensitive
- any exact "X% cheaper than Starbucks" claim needs date and location discipline

What seems safe:

- use exact comparisons only when you manually audit a specific local market on a specific date
- store the competitor, location, audit date, and source URL or screenshot
- treat delivery app pricing separately from in-store pricing

What seems unsafe:

- claiming a timeless national price difference
- comparing your in-store price to a competitor's delivery price
- using stale screenshots as if they are current

### What kind of comparison content is actually good

Price is only one axis. Better comparison content would include:

- sweetness level
- portion size
- texture
  - gooey
  - cakey
  - crisp edge
  - soft center
- best occasion
  - solo treat
  - coffee break
  - gifting
  - office box
  - church event
  - dessert after dinner
- pairings
  - coffee
  - milk
  - iced drink
- style
  - comforting
  - homemade
  - rich
  - playful
  - over-the-top

This gives you a much more credible comparison experience than price alone.

### Messaging idea: when would someone choose us vs them?

This section is inference, not something the competitors say about themselves.

Choose Baked with Blessings when the customer wants:

- something that feels more homemade than mass-chain
- a simpler menu and less decision fatigue
- strong comfort-food energy
- a local or community-rooted feel
- a treat that fits coffee, brunch, church, family, or gifting without feeling too corporate

Choose Starbucks when the customer wants:

- speed and convenience
- coffee first, bakery second
- rewards habit and predictable pickup
- lots of drink customization
- a familiar national chain experience

Choose Crumbl when the customer wants:

- dessert drama
- novelty and weekly flavor chasing
- oversized, highly indulgent cookies
- shareable boxes for groups
- giftable, photogenic packaging

### What this suggests for the website

Product comparison content should probably answer:

1. Why this item exists.
2. Who it is best for.
3. What kind of competitor item it replaces.
4. Whether your advantage is price, portion, ingredient quality, flavor style, or occasion fit.

Good examples:

- "If you like Crumbl for shareable boxes but want something less sugary, start here."
- "If you usually grab Starbucks with coffee, this is our better coffee-pairing cookie."
- "Best for office sharing."
- "Best for soft-cookie people."
- "Best when you want homemade comfort, not a giant dessert bomb."
