# Cookie sheep rig alignment history

This note exists so future AI does not restart the sheep-alignment work from the wrong mental model. The early attempts treated the head, tail, and legs as decorative pieces positioned by ad hoc percentages against the card. That produced something that could look acceptable on one screen size, but it was brittle. As soon as the cookie size changed, or the card width changed on mobile, the limbs detached, the head floated too far away, and every new adjustment felt like fighting the viewport rather than composing a body. The important lesson is that the cookie itself has to become the coordinate system.

The first real breakthrough was to treat the cookie as a normalized circle and to express attachment points as clock positions measured in milliseconds from `12:00`. That changed design feedback from vague offset talk into precise joint language. Instead of saying "move the leg down and a little inward," we could say "attach this pair closer to `7:20` and point it toward `6:00`." The geometry layer in `cookie-sheep-geometry.ts` is the result of that shift. `clockTimeToMs`, `clockMsToDegrees`, and `placePartOnCookie(...)` are not abstraction for abstraction's sake; they exist because clock language turned out to be the only stable way to tune the pose across flavors and viewport changes.

The second breakthrough was realizing that moving the part container is not the same as moving the visible art. The head kept looking far away from the cookie even after its placement box was brought inward, because the SVG itself had internal empty space. That is why the rig now has two passes: `placePartOnCookie(...)` positions the box relative to the cookie body, and `placePartArt(...)` shifts and scales the art inside that box. If a future edit makes the head or tail look detached, do not immediately blame the joint math. First inspect whether the asset has empty bounds. In practice, `radialOffsetPct` changes the box-to-cookie distance, while `artTranslateXPct`, `artTranslateYPct`, and `artScale` change the visible pixel relationship inside that box. That distinction is what finally made "close to touching the cookie" possible.

The third important lesson was sizing from the body instead of from the scene. The cookie needs to be normalized first, then the head, tail, and leg spacing should be derived from that body. `CookieBodyGeometryService` exists for that reason. Once head size, tail size, and pair spacing were computed as percentages of the cookie body rather than guessed from the card, the composition stopped drifting as badly between cookies. If alignment ever feels wrong again, start by checking whether the cookie body size changed in `cookie-poster-grid.tsx` or the image scale changed in `cookie-sheep-rig.tsx`. Tune body size first, then tune parts. Trying to fix a body-scale problem with limb offsets leads to wasted work.

The legs also taught a specific lesson: asset reuse was not automatically better. Reusing the old hoof SVGs sounded efficient, but they carried visual baggage and made the pose harder to control. The final direction became custom SVG leg shapes driven by the same joint math as the other parts. The practical rule is that each leg pair should have two independent ideas: where the pair attaches on the cookie rim, and what direction the leg art points. Those are not the same thing. Attachment uses the clock joint, while orientation uses either `directionClockMs` or explicit rotation logic. When the sheep needs to feel like it is running, that usually means the pair attaches near one clock region but points somewhere else. Treating those as separate variables is what made the pose tunable instead of fragile.

The final sweet spot came from tuning in a strict order rather than touching random numbers. First normalize the cookie body size. Second place the head and tail by clock joint and radial offset. Third correct visible closeness with art-local transforms if the SVG canvas is misleading. Fourth place the leg pairs by joint clock, then adjust their direction separately so the motion reads correctly. Fifth adjust pair spacing only after the joint locations are right. Sixth, if the hover interaction needs work, leave the placement math alone and tune the burst vectors in the rig plus the shared hover CSS in `cookie-poster-grid.tsx`. That separation of concerns is deliberate. Geometry decides where parts live at rest; art-local transforms decide how the visible pixels sit inside their boxes; hover animation decides how parts leave. Future edits should respect that layering instead of collapsing everything back into one-off offsets.
